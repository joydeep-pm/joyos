# AOP vs Roadmap

## Why this note exists

The strategy sources contain both narrative ambition and structured planning math. This note keeps the FY27 plan honest by focusing on scope, sequencing, concentration, and execution risk.

## Planning scale

Across the source corpus, the FY27 investment is described as roughly:
- **~5,385 PD** in the strategy report / c-suite snapshot framing
- **~5,551 PD** in the HTML / revised AOP framing

This difference is not the main issue. The main issue is concentration and execution quality.

## What the AOP is trying to do

The annual plan is not just a feature list. It attempts to do five jobs in one year:
- close major parity gaps
- remove compliance vetoes
- enter new markets
- improve ecosystem leverage
- preserve product quality through repeated stabilization cycles

## Quarter logic

### Q1 — Close the gap
Primary purpose:
- fix the most commercially painful parity gaps
- improve integration/config credibility
- protect Gold and LAS/LAMF from being crowded out

### Q2 — Enterprise readiness
Primary purpose:
- remove compliance and audit objections
- deepen collections/recovery/legal readiness
- extend parity into adjacent categories

### Q3 — B2B and international bets
Primary purpose:
- vehicle and lease depth
- Islamic finance entry
- capital markets capability
- residency / regional readiness where relevant

### Q4 — Monetize ecosystem and differentiation
Primary purpose:
- AI premiumization
- ecosystem leverage
- broader packaging and platform monetization

## Capacity concentration

The workbook resource plan shows that risk is not evenly distributed.

Largest visible concentration areas include:
- Product — New Asset Classes
- Quality / Operations
- Ecosystem & Integration
- Accounting / Finance
- Capital Markets

### Key implication
The plan can fail even if strategy direction is correct, simply because too much critical work clusters into a few constrained execution zones.

## Quality as protected capacity

One of the strongest signals in the source corpus is that bug bashes / bug-a-thons are not optional cleanup exercises.

They are part of the commercial strategy because:
- referenceability matters
- trust matters
- implementation quality affects new sales
- unstable execution will erase parity wins

## Dependency logic that leadership should watch

### 1. Gold and LAS need protected Q1 bandwidth
These appear to be among the clearest parity recovery bets.

### 2. Co-lending can absorb disproportionate attention
It is strategically valuable, but also one of the easiest places for complexity to expand and crowd out other priorities.

### 3. Config and integration quality are upstream revenue dependencies
These are not back-office platform concerns. They affect demo quality, setup confidence, and build-vs-buy outcomes.

### 4. New-market bets depend on specialist capability
Capital markets and Islamic finance both require domain confidence, not just engineering effort.

## Leadership reading

The right question is not “is the roadmap large?”

The right questions are:
- which work is commercially mandatory?
- which work is concentration risk?
- which work must be protected from adjacent complexity?
- what breaks if a given quarter slips?

## Practical summary

The AOP is viable only if leadership enforces focus.

That means:
- protecting parity recovery work
- not downgrading compliance gates
- ring-fencing stabilization capacity
- making domain hiring part of roadmap delivery, not a side action
